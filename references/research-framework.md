# Serenity Research Framework

## Core Questions

Use these questions to keep the research grounded:

- What secular demand trend is creating physical stress?
- Which layer of the value chain has the slowest capacity response?
- Why is the constraint hard to solve: capex, qualification, IP, process know-how, regulation, geology, geopolitics, or customer conservatism?
- Which public companies have direct economic exposure to that layer?
- What evidence shows that demand or pricing is already moving toward the company?
- What would falsify the thesis within 3, 6, and 12 months?

## Bottleneck Map Pattern

For each trend, map at least three layers beyond the obvious mega-cap beneficiaries.

Example AI infrastructure layers:

- Compute: GPUs, ASICs, accelerators, HBM, advanced packaging.
- Networking: optical transceivers, lasers, DSPs, switches, connectors, fiber.
- Photonics and substrates: InP, GaAs, SOI wafers, silicon photonics, epitaxy.
- Manufacturing tools: deposition, metrology, lithography-adjacent tools, wafer-level test, burn-in.
- Packaging and boards: IC substrates, ABF, PCB materials, glass fiber, thermal interface materials.
- Data-center physical plant: power equipment, transformers, switchgear, cooling, backup generation.
- Upstream materials: specialty gases, chemicals, quartz, ceramics, rare metals, high-purity consumables.

Do not stop at this list. Use it as a scaffold, then adapt to the user-specified trend.

## Candidate Scoring Rubric

Score 1-5 for each category. A strong candidate usually needs high exposure and a tolerable bear case, not a perfect total score.

| Category | 1 | 3 | 5 |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Bottleneck purity | Mostly unrelated revenue | Some segment exposure | Core revenue tied to scarce layer |
| Scarcity | Many easy substitutes | Moderate supplier concentration | Few qualified suppliers or hard capacity |
| Customer validation | Hype only | Pilots or indirect references | Named customers, design wins, or qualification evidence |
| Revenue timing | No clear path | Possible in 12-24 months | Orders/backlog/guidance already turning |
| Operating leverage | Low margin or capex-heavy | Some scale benefits | Small revenue changes can move earnings materially |
| Balance sheet | Near-term financing risk | Manageable runway | Strong cash/runway for thesis period |
| Valuation setup | Thesis fully priced | Mixed | Market appears to underprice exposure |
| Liquidity/structure | Very illiquid/promotional | Tradable but volatile | Adequate liquidity and clean structure |
| Bear-case severity | Single-point failure | Material risks | Risks visible and monitorable |

## Evidence Hierarchy

Prefer stronger evidence over social-media narratives:

1. SEC filings, annual reports, 10-Q/10-K, S-1/F-1, 8-K, proxy statements.
2. Earnings calls, investor presentations, backlog/order disclosures, capex plans.
3. Customer/supplier announcements and technical qualification notices.
4. Patent filings, standards participation, government procurement, permits, import/export data.
5. Credible industry publications and analyst/channel checks.
6. Social posts, forums, newsletters, and promotional writeups. Use these as leads, not proof.

## Falsification Checklist

Before presenting a bullish thesis, actively look for:

- Substitute technologies with shorter qualification cycles.
- Larger competitors adding capacity faster.
- Customer dual-sourcing or internalizing the component.
- Revenue exposure too small to matter.
- Gross margins inconsistent with claimed pricing power.
- Backlog that is cancellable, low quality, or unrelated.
- Debt maturity, going-concern language, ATM offerings, convertibles, warrants, or repeated dilution.
- Insider selling, related-party transactions, promotional IR, or auditor changes.
- Export controls, sanctions, tariffs, customer geography, or supplier concentration.
- Valuation already assuming best-case growth.

## Deep-Dive Memo Template

Use this format for one company:

```markdown
# [Ticker] Research Memo

## One-Sentence Thesis
[If demand for X strains Y, company Z may benefit because...]

## Supply-Chain Role
[Where the company sits, what it sells, and why that layer is constrained.]

## Evidence
- [Source-backed fact]
- [Source-backed fact]

## What the Market May Be Missing
[The research gap: overlooked segment, small revenue base, lagged financial recognition, hidden customer exposure, etc.]

## Catalysts and Timing
[Events that could reveal or invalidate the thesis.]

## Valuation Sanity Check
[Simple revenue/earnings/EV comparison; avoid false precision.]

## Bear Case
[Strongest reasons the thesis fails.]

## Diligence Questions
- [Question]
- [Question]

## Bottom Line
[Research conclusion with confidence level; avoid personalized advice.]
```

## Language Guidelines

- Use "candidate," "watchlist," "thesis," and "risk" rather than "must buy."
- State confidence as low/medium/high and explain what would change it.
- When evidence is incomplete, say so plainly.
- Do not overfit to past multi-bagger examples. The goal is a repeatable research process, not copying another trader's trade.
